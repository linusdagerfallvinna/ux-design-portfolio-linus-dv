Internal Configuration System

1. Intro

I have worked for Qliro AB, which is a leading fintech company in the Nordics. Qliro AB is a payment solution that enables merchants to offer their customers Qliro's payment method when they shop. When private individuals choose one of Qliro's payment methods when shopping at, for example, CAIA or Biltema, they receive an invoice that they can pay in the Qliro app or on the Qliro website. I have designed an internal configuration system to make the onboarding process for Qliro's merchants more efficient and user-friendly. I was expected to hand over an MVP to the developers with a design that creates business value.


During this time I have worked in a team that focuses on creating a good experience for the merchants who sign with Qliro. The project I will go through here has to do with the process that takes place after the merchant enters into an agreement with Qliro to be able to start using Qliro's payment method. My assignment during that project was to design an interface for an internal system that would be used to ease the process of getting a merchant launched after signing.

Through interviews I collected the needs of the internal stakeholders that could be relevant for this new platform. I also read old documentation such as a competitor analysis that was done before. I worked closely with the developers and product department where it was me and another designer working through all 5 phases of the design process. Sometimes we collaborated by generating ideas for proposed solutions, but most of the time we divided the work in the project and worked independently.

The problem

How can we create a system that makes the onboarding process more efficient and user-friendly?

2. Empathize

Interviews

I aimed to gather insights and opinions from different stakeholders who were in different phases of the customer journey. In addition, I was interested in deepening my understanding of the business. To achieve this goal, I had a meeting with the product manager to gain an understanding of which stakeholders could be relevant for interviews.

After that, I booked in various stakeholders internally at Qliro for interviews to gather information about what everyone was doing in each department, what their goals and motivations were, and if they had any problems in their day-to-day work. I interviewed 7 different stakeholders. Based on the interviews, I was able to define which stakeholders would be relevant to the project and the new system.

3. Define

After I had collected information from the interviews, I used Figma Jam to compile and categorize this data. This method enabled us to gain a better understanding of the views and feelings of students and staff, as well as to identify problem areas. I then translated the findings to my english speaking colleague in a table.

The table had these columns:

  • Relevance
  • Contact person
  • Goals
  • Needs
  • JTBD
  • Motivations
  • Challanges
  • Pain points
  • Other info
  • User stories

    • Personas & User Journey Map

    In a tool called app.theydo.com, I was able to easily create the personas that were relevant to the project. In the same tool, I was able to connect all the personas into a customer journey map. In the customer journey map, I added the phases that the onboarding included today. I did not have to work with only one persona in the customer journey map, but have been able to include everyone in an easy way. This made it easier to get an overview of the onboardinging process.

    4. Ideate

    Sketches

    I then started sketching ideas around the flow and layout together with my colleague. We used a whiteboard and paper and pencil. It felt like this session turned our insights into ideas and it helped us get a clearer picture of the project and how to take the next step.

    Low fi-prototype v.2:

    The next step was to work with simple sketches in Figma to come up with a design concept. These are usually called low fi prototypes and were very simple and not built using components. We tested different proposals where the goal was to try to get as many ideas as possible that could solve the problems that the stakeholders are struggling with today. It was important to also keep the project's goals and purpose in mind.

    The idea was to have a horizontal menu at the top. On the left I added a vertical menu with the different traders. The idea was to divide the traders into different sections in the menu depending on which phase of the onboarding the trader was in. For each merchant, I sketched out a detailed view that I placed in the center. In this view, work tasks for the employees in the Integration department were presented.

    The view contained input fields where some fields were already auto-populated and some needed to be filled in. I also displayed information about what stage of the process the merchant was in and how far along they had come. I tried having a search field at the top as well as in the vertical menu on the left.

    When I felt that I had come up with many variants, I started to build a prototype of the entire flow. This time using components to keep views continuous and to facilitate changes. Although we used components, we only focused on making the flow user-friendly, so the components kept a primitive look without thinking about colors and real fonts. We used a gray scale and fonts reminiscent of handwriting. This made it easier for us to focus only on the user experience and the position of the various components.

    The appearance of these prototypes almost looked like the first sketches, although we began to cut down on features that did not fit into the first version due to time constraints.

    5. Prototype (Hi-fi)

    We then started creating High fi prototypes. Similar to the work with the low fi prototypes, we started sketching out a look without components to get a common vision of what it could look like.

    Then we also created components for high-fi:

    We made a lot of different versions. Among other things, we tested having the steps for the onboarding horizontal instead of vertical.

    I then started creating a bunch of different versions of the steps and testing them against each other. My goal was to try to combine the name of the step with a status icon as well as show where in the process the user was.

    I was assigned to work intensively on the login page to get it ready for development. We chose to add an animation at the top with logos of existing merchants to motivate Qliro's staff. As those using the system are working on getting merchants launched I thought it would give them a positive feeling before they log into the system.

    Then I continued working on the views for logged in mode. I created a new overview of all traders and removed the menu we previously had on the left as it was not needed for the first version.

    The MVP became more and more limited. Due to lack of time and the fact that we had to deliver something to the developers. Therefore, we received instructions from the managers that we should remove some functions from the first version.

    6. Test

    When we had prepared the prototypes for development, we wanted to ensure that they would also work in practice. Because of that I booked proper user tests with 4 people at "Integration". These are the people who will be using the system in its first release. Before the test, I did research on different techniques for user testing. I did this because I needed to get a better understanding of what type of test would be appropriate for our particular session. I decided to do a concept test where the participants would perform tasks wrapped in scenarios.

    Then I started writing a script for the user test to plan what I would say and do. In the script I explained who I was, what the project was about and that the product they were going to test was a first version. I explained the purpose of the test and how it would be done with the help of a powerpoint presentation. The test consisted of 2 parts.

    First part where I let the participant think of different scenarios where they would perform some tasks. We used the "think out loud method" where I asked them to think out loud about how they solved the task. To make the test as effective as possible, the users had to imagine scenarios where they had to perform tasks for all views in the system. In this way, we could test the entire system in a natural way.

    In the second part, the focus was on asking the participants about their experience during the test and if they could give examples of what could be improved.

    I am satisfied with the results of the user test. During all the sessions, the participants were very engaged and gave me good feedback on what worked well today and what could be improved. The questions I asked the user after they had completed the tasks helped me gain a better understanding of the overall experience. Because I recorded the user test, it was easier for me to take notes afterwards. It also became easier to observe the user's actions in the system. In summary, it was easy for most participants to perform all the tasks despite having never used the system before.

    After the test, I watched and listened through all the recordings and began to record my observations regarding all the tasks the test subjects had performed. I then wrote down positive and negative things that the users had expressed during the test, as well as observations from their actions. I also wrote down the answers to the questions I asked them at the end.

    Then I wrote down findings on post-it notes where I used one color per person and one finding per note. I grouped these notes into piles to more easily see which had more post-its than others. I tried to divide these piles into three parts. Positive feedback, negative feedback and other info.

    Through this method, it became easier to see what kind of groupings there were and what had more notes. I then wrote a list of the most important findings from the user test.

    7. Next steps

    Now that I have completed my user tests and analyzed the data, the team will prioritize what needs to be changed in the system to then develop a working MVP. It will be easier to prioritize it because of the grouping I made where it is easier to find patterns. Once the MVP is developed, it will be tested in the test environment in order to further adjust it for the better. Then, functions will gradually be added to the configuration system. These features will also be tested and tweaked before they reach end users.

    8. Lessons learned & reflection

    During this project I have learned a lot from working through all 5 phases of the design process. Among other things, I have realized how important it is to be extra clear and not assume that everyone perceives things in the same way.

    Although this work has been fairly problem-free, some difficulties have nevertheless arisen. When I was going to perform my user tests, some of the ones I tested were not in the office as I expected. I should have been more clear in telling them that it works better if you have a physical meeting. However, it didn't become such a big problem.
    Several times during the project we received new directives from above, which meant that many of the prototypes we worked with were not used. It was a rather complex project, which led to some people misunderstanding each other and good communication was particularly important.

    We received positive feedback from our test session that the new system was more user-friendly and efficient than previous systems, which was the goal of the project. We also received feedback on improvements which will mean adjustments in this new system which will then be validated with users in the future. I am satisfied with the project because we received feedback that indicates that we worked in the right direction.

    Feedback tool
    Qliro AB

    1. Intro

    I have worked on a project at Qliro during my internship related to feedback. Qliro is a payment solution that enables merchants to offer their customers Qliro's payment method when they shop. When private individuals choose one of Qliro's payment methods when shopping at, for example, CAIA or Biltema, they receive an invoice that they can pay in the Qliro app or on the Qliro website. I have done research on multiple tools meant for feedback to find the most fitting one for the company.

    My first week I was introduced to a project that was about feedback. I was informed that there were some problem areas with the internal feedback at Qliro. My mission was to find a new tool to solve these problems.

    2. Empathize

    To get a better understanding of what didn't work, I organized a workshop for various stakeholders in the product department. The workshop was divided into 3 parts where they individually had to write down on post-it notes what did not work, what did work and requirements for the new tool. I then had them post the notes on a common board. After each part, they had to present their notes, which resulted in a very good discussion between the different participants. I recorded the workshop to make it easier to take notes afterwards.

    3. Define

    After the workshop, I collected the notes and listened through the entire recording. I decoded the patches in Figma Jam and created different groupings for requirements and problems.

    The problems today:

  • No communication back
  • No common place to collect feedback
  • The feedback gets forgotten about
  • Not enough investigation of the feedback
  • Unstructured and uncategorised feedback
  • No good overview of the feedback

    • I then created a requirements list to get an overview of the participants' needs.

    4. Desktop Research

    I started comparing different tools against each other to find the one that suited Qliro best. During the workshop, I received many good recommendations for tools that I could look into. The list of requirements was used as a checklist during my research where I could check off the things that matched. Among other tools, I tested Airtable, Canny, Product Board and Aureliuslab. After my work I could see which tool had the most checks.

    5. Result

    I could tell that one tool stood out, Jira. First, my plan was to use the existing Jira Service Management tool, which offered an easy way to enter feedback. Through a form. However, this tool was not available to use for the planned amount of people without any extra cost. I had a meeting where the price was discussed and we came to the conclusion that we had to continue with the next best option, Jira Work Management.

    Unfortunately, this tool did not have the same features as Jira Service Management. Among other things, it was not possible to adapt the questions to what the user had previously answered. This function would have been very useful as you don't want to show unnecessary questions that don't concern everyone. Therefore, I needed to focus a lot on creating fairly general questions in the form and structuring it to fit all types of feedback. I am satisfied with the result where the users can submit feedback in an easy way through a form where the feedback is compiled on a kanban board.

    6. Test

    I had a meeting with some people who work with support for the merchants to find out if they had any feedback to report on the platforms they are working with today. They shared very valuable feedback which I then fed into the form to test that the tool was well suited for real feedback. It was important that the choices in the drop down lists had the right options and that all feedback could be reported without having to write everything in free text only.

    Later I presented the tool to those who had previously attended the workshop to ensure that I was working in the right direction. They appreciated it but also gave some suggestions on what could be improved. I tweaked the tool and then presented it to several departments where they had to use the tool to submit existing feedback.

    7. Reflection

    If I had to do it over, I would have chosen to spend more time on the research. It felt like it went really fast and I missed some stuff because of it. Therefore, some adjustments had to be made that would not have been necessary otherwise.

    I am satisfied with the project as I have managed to produce a tool that fits the requirements so well. From now on, it will be faster to send in structured feedback to a single place where you can see all the feedback at a glance. With the help of continuous meetings, the feedback will no longer be forgotten. At these meetings, the feedback will be discussed before it is prioritized into the roadmap. Now it is easier to see who reported the feedback from the beginning, which is important in case you have any questions.

    The requirements of Däckskiftaren

    Requirements management of "Däckskiftaren

    1. Intro and working method

    We were given an assignment to create a digital service that would facilitate tire changes for private individuals and workshops by the fictitious investors HarMycketPengar AB. The investment company identified a need in the market for private individuals to be able to book a change of tires for the winter and summer season and therefore hired us. The service would be educational and easy to use and would allow private individuals to book an appointment with the nearest workshop for a tire change. In addition to that, there was free interpretation on how we would solve this problem. Once inside the project, it expanded and we needed to find a way to make money through the service all year round.

    Through interviews, we gathered the needs of the various stakeholders. We interviewed private individuals, the investors, an administrator and a car mechanic. In addition to that, we also used other requirements collection techniques such as e.g. surveys, desktop research and competitor analysis. We also created a needs map. We worked a lot together and used tools such as Miro, Jira and Figma.

    Based on the overall needs, we identified requirements which we then broke down into smaller parts and created a product backlog for the team to work with. It was important to have focus on UX and to include the most important from the perspective of the stakeholders when we documented the requirements. At the end of the project, we presented our work with a focus on our MVP and the prototype that visualized our vision. We then also highlighted what we learned and our success factors.

    User research

    The survey that we sent out to private individuals was divided into two parts so that we could distinguish answers from people with a car from people without a car. In the survey, we wanted, among other things examine how willing people are to download apps and how they typically make appointments. The result showed that most prefer to book via an app. We received a total of 42 responses from the survey.

    In the interview, we asked the participants what plays a role in the choice of workshop. Many then answered that proximity was very important. This helped us move forward in many discussions during the work. It felt reassuring to constantly think about what we found out from our research in order not to accidentally create something that no one needs.

    2. User Story Map & MVP

    With the help of User story mapping, we were able to visualize the prioritization of our requirements and come up with an MVP. To get a good structure, we divided the requirements into epics, features and user stories. In our User story map, the post-it notes visualize our user stories.

    Version 1:

    We made the first User Story Map during class time. Then we visualized our requirements and came up with one MVP divided into epics, features and user stories.

    Version 2:

    When we realized that we wouldn't have time to do much in the first release, we had to cut down the number of user stories in our MVP. This meant that we needed to redo our User Story Maps.

    We chose to remove, among other things, the login and focus more on the booking system itself. Since we rewrote the User stories so that they would be easy to understand and included the number from Jira so we could find our way back to the original user story. In this version we sorted our User Story Map from left to right in a way that mimics the user's customer journey through the app.

    Lessons Learned: Not too many requirements in the MVP. Important to make it easy to understand.

    3. Sitemap

    We created a sitemap to get a quick overview of the flow of the app and what each category should contain.

    To get a clear overview of the app and its various pages, we created a Sitemap. With the help of it, it became easier to see how everything would fit together and which views we needed to create prototypes for. It may seem unnecessary, but it was a good skeleton to start from.

    4. Jira & Sprintplaning

    We used Jira to structure our requirements and worked a lot with tags to do it is easy to understand.

    Our backlog:

    An example of how we divided the requirements in Jira. We divided them into Epics, Features and User stories. We chose to plan sprints according to different groups that had to do with each other. We gave story points to each story and also a priority.

    Sprint 1:

    After this sprint, the customer should be able to book an appointment in the app and information will be sent to the CRM system.

    Sprint 2:

    After this sprint, the customer should be able to search and filter as well as receive reminders and notifications.

    We used the gherkin method to specify acceptance criteria for our user stories but also tried writing some test cases.

    After our work in Jira, we felt that we had learned how to write different types of acceptance criteria, how to write a good user story, how Jira works and how to do sprint planning. We also learned about the difference between epics, features and user stories.

    5. Prototype

    We chose to make the service as an app and we wanted to be able to offer different services to private individuals at home. At first, the plan was to focus only on tire changes, but when the requirements changed, we expanded it with, among other things, recond and repairing stone ships.

    View prototype



    While working on the prototype, we realized that it was good to have a vote in cases we couldn't agree. It helped us not get stuck for too long. We divided the work in a good way so that not too many people sat with the same thing. Before we started prototyping, we tried to agree on a common style. Then it became easier to continue the work individually.

    6. Lessons learned & reflection

    During the work, we have learned to try to avoid getting stuck in discussions and not drift away on side tracks. As there were many people who want to make their voice heard, it may take time to come forward. Because we chose to vote on different decisions. We also learned that it is important to double-check the survey before sending it out to the public. We happened to send it out when it contained some errors which resulted in some useless answers. When we were going to document the requirements, we tested using Miro, which we didn't realize suited so well for that particular purpose. However, it is well suited for brainstorming. In Miro we also learned to use frames and name them well so you can search for them.

    During the work with the prototypes, we chose to test the prototype on users. We let the users perform various tasks in the app. We observed in the meantime and took notes. These user tests helped us improve the app further.

    Redesign of Chas Academy

    Redesign of Chas Academy

    1. Empathize

    We were given a task to develop a prototype for a website. We chose to redesign our school's website (Chas Academy AB). We focused on the desktop version but we also made 3 detailed wireframes for mobile. The purpose of the assignment was for us to get used to work with the phases of the Design Thinking Process. From researching the users' needs to testing our prototypes with the users.

    We started to formulate a problem statement to get a clearer picture of which problem should be investigated and solved. Because we wanted to find out how to best shape a website that attracts students, we came up with the problem statement: "How do we get more people to choose Chas Academy over other schools?". Then we tried to expand our knowledge of the area by checking out the existing Chas Academy site as well as other similar sites.

    User research

    When we were done with the interview guide and had written the questions for the survey, we invited acquaintances to an interview and sent the survey out on social media so that it could be out over the weekend.

    During the time that the survey was out there were 21 people who answered it. Due to the limited time in our school project, we had to settle for these answers although the statistics would have been more credible with more participants.

    We then interviewed our participants and diligently recorded and documented what they said.

    From the data we received from the research, it was possible to see, among other things, that people looking for a school want a website that looks professional, has good search functions and contains easily accessible information as well as links to the school portal. It was also possible to see that the vast majority could imagine applying for an IT education without previous experience.

    The Chas Academy website received mostly negative reviews when we showed it during the interviews. The participants felt that the page did not match their expectations of a school website that has an UX-design course. But it also received some positive reactions. The reactions of the participants are shown below:

    Painpoints:

  • Expected more
  • Does not represent the UX-design course
  • Missing color
  • Need more info about the courses
  • Too much whitespaces at the logo
  • Need more pictures
  • Too much text
  • Need more info about the school at HOME
  • Main menu is too big
  • Unnecessary arrows on the slide show
  • Need more references
  • Hard to find contact info

    • Positive feedback:

  • Easy to navigate
  • Easy to get to "How to apply"
  • Elegant website
  • The website is structured

    • 2. Define

    After our research, we first compiled our notes into documents and then wrote down relevant findings on post-it notes. We then put them up on a whiteboard one by one and grouped the ones we thought fit together.

    We came up with 6 different groupings: Choice of school, personal information about the user, school attendance at a university of applied sciences, method of study, further education and positive/negative feedback on today's website. Then we chose to prioritize the 3 most important groupings. We chose to prioritize: Positive/negative feedback on today's website, choice of school and method of study.



    Here is the result of our affinity diagram. Using this method, it became much clearer what needs to be done to create a website that meets the users' needs.

    Create personas

    Based on the "personal info" grouping in our affinity diagram, we were able to distinguish patterns and common factors among users. We created two personas. Patrick the ambitious and active 39-year-old who works in IT and the 25-year-old student Liam who is caring and stubborn.



    3. Ideate

    In the third phase, we unleash creativity and work together to get different perspectives and suggestions for solutions. This phase is called Ideate. It is important to never dismiss an idea. Even an idea that seems bad can lead to a new angle of approach.

    After we defined the target group and the challenge, it was time to start generating ideas for a solution. We arranged a workshop where everyone in the group had to outline suggestions that can solve the challenges with today's website. After the workshop, we collected all the sketches and compared them with each other. We then tried to agree on what we wanted to proceed with.

    Here you see an example of how the first sketches looked. At this stage we came up with the idea of showing all the school's courses at HOME. We also tried to answer the users' need for more moving material on the website by having videos about the school.

    When we ideated, we worked with a “mobile-first” approach, as it is becoming more and more common to surf via mobile these days. Therefore, we outlined solutions for both mobile and desktop. Here is a sitemap that represents the hierarchy of the new site, what sections are on each page and how to get from one page to another. We have added the "Inför studier"-page, which did not exist before.



    4. Prototype

    To visualize our ideas in physical form, we created a prototype for mobile and one for desktop. The desktop hifi prototype can be seen by clicking on the image below.

    View prototype

    We first started working with wireframes to show how the idea fits together and determine the position of the various components. Then a more detailed hi-fi prototype was created that better represents our vision of the school's new website. We made the prototype clickable, which became useful later during user testing.

    5. Test

    To test our solutions, we booked user tests with the users. We wrote a usability testing script together. With the script, we planned all the tasks that the user needed to complete during the user test to test the website. We performed 7 pieces of scenario-based user tests where the user had to live into different scenarios in order to perform tasks. We ended with a few short questions.

    After the test, we compiled everyone's notes and looked for patterns and common factors. We got a more positive impression of our updated version. Those who were familiar with how the site looked before found the new version to be more inspiring. Users also appreciated our new page "Inför studier", which compiled everything that might be good to think about before starting school.

    There was also room for improvement. Among other things, we saw that people were sometimes bothered by the fact that there was too much text on the website. This resulted in too much scrolling on the pages. The body text was also too large in some places. By observing the users, we noticed that they were looking for "important dates" on the "Inför studier"-page instead of on the "Så ansöker du"-page where it actually was. Beyond what I mentioned, we received a lot of useful information that can be used to further improve the prototype.
